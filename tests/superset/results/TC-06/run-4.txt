**2. Rewritten version**

Good writing is hard. Most people skip the hard part.

They open with "In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape" and call it a day. But that phrase tells the reader nothing. It's noise. The same goes for "it's important to note that" — if it's important, just say it. Don't announce you're about to say it.

Structure matters more than most people realize. A well-organized piece does two things: it makes a point, and it makes sure the reader follows along. Hook, body, close. That's it. You don't need a "journey" metaphor. You need a clear line from A to B.

The best writers I know share one habit: they revise. Not once, not twice — until the thing says what they meant. That's not a trifecta. That's just doing the work.

Clichés are the enemy here. "Pen mightier than the sword" — we've heard it. "Journey, not a destination" — heard it too. If you catch yourself writing something you've read a hundred times, cut it. Your reader has read it a hundred times too.

Write like you're talking to someone who's busy. They don't want a tour. They want the point.
