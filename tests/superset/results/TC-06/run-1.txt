## 2. Rewritten version

Good communication matters more than most people realize. How you say something — in an email, a memo, a blog post — can determine whether anyone reads it, let alone acts on it.

Structure is the part people skip. They focus on word choice, on finding the perfect verb, on making each sentence sing. But a wall of beautiful sentences still fails if there's no shape underneath. A good piece has a hook, a middle that earns its keep, and an ending that lands. Every paragraph should do one job and then get out of the way.

The best writers I know share one habit: they rewrite. Not once, not twice — they keep going until the piece says what they meant, not just what they typed. That's it. No secret formula, no trifecta, no magic ratio. Just the willingness to kill your darlings and start over.

So write something. Then cut forty percent of it. Then read it out loud. If you stumble, fix that sentence. Repeat until you don't stumble anymore.

## 5. Ethics note

These are signals, not proof. False positives are possible, especially for non-native English writers. The patterns flagged here are statistically more common in LLM output, but humans on autopilot produce the same shapes. Pair the signal with context.
