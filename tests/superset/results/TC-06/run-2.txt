## Rewritten version

Good writing is harder than it looks. Most people think it's about vocabulary or grammar. It's not. It's about structure — how you lead someone from one idea to the next without losing them.

Take the opening. If you start with "In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape," you've already lost half your readers. They've seen that sentence a hundred times. They know nothing new is coming. A better opener: say something specific. Name a thing. Take a position.

Structure matters more than most people realize. A well-organized piece does two things: it makes the writer look like they know what they're talking about, and it makes the reader feel smart for following along. That's the whole game. Every paragraph should earn its place. If you can delete it and nothing breaks, delete it.

I've been writing long enough to know that the real trick isn't fancy transitions or clever metaphors. It's rewriting. The first draft is just you figuring out what you think. The second draft is where the actual writing happens. Most people stop too early.

So here's the short version: write something. Cut half of it. Rewrite what's left. Then do it again. That's not a formula — it's just how the work gets done.

## Ethics note

These are signals, not proof. False positives are possible, especially for non-native English writers. The patterns flagged here are statistically more common in LLM output, but humans under deadline pressure or writing in unfamiliar genres produce the same shapes. Pair the signal with context.
