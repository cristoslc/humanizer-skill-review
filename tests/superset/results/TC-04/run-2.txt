## Rewritten version

I've been thinking about writing. Not the kind people sell you courses on. Just writing, the kind where you say what you mean.

Here's the thing. Most people think good writing is about rules. It's not. It's about reading. Read enough good writing and you stop needing rules. You just know.

Write a lot. Cut most of it. The stuff that's left, that's where the good stuff lives.

## What changed

- Cut the AI opener, filler, and inflation entirely — started with a concrete first-person statement matching the sample
- Replaced the false-concession structure with a direct "Here's the thing" (matches sample's "The thing is")
- Replaced the rule-of-three and self-labeling ("trifecta") with a single claim
- Shortened every sentence to match the sample's ~6-7 word average
- Used fragments, contractions, and first person throughout
- Zero em dashes

## Second-pass audit

Clean. No remaining tells. Sentence lengths match the sample (all under 10 words). Voice is consistent — same register, same rhythm, same directness. No hedging, no transitions, no inflation.

## Ethics note

These are signals, not proof. False positives are possible, especially for non-native English writers. The patterns flagged here are statistically more common in LLM output, but humans writing under deadline pressure or in a second language produce the same shapes.
